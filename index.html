Profile

Myers-Briggs Style Personality Test

The 16 Personalities Test is an online questionnaire that attempts to measure differing preferences in how people percieve the world and make decisions. Results are based on four categories, plus an identity type. The combination of these results determines assignment to one of sixteen possible personality types. The scales measured are:

  • Extraversion (E) vs Introversion (I) - How a person gets their energy
  • Intuitive (N) vs Observant (S) - What a person does with the information gathered from the world around them
  • Thinking (T) vs Feeling (F) - How a person makes decisions
  • Judging (J) vs Perceiving (P) - How a person organizes their world.
  • Assertive (A) vs Turbulent (T) - This identity scale affects all others and indicates how confident we are in our abilities and decisions

My Personality Type is Defender - ISFJ-T
The Defender personality type is quite unique, as many of their qualities defy the definition of their individual traits. Though sensitive, Defenders have excellent analytical abilities; though reserved, they have well-developed people skills and robust social relationships; and though they are generally a conservative type, Defenders are often receptive to change and new ideas. As with so many things, people with the Defender personality type are more than the sum of their parts, and it is the way they use these strengths that defines who they are.

I - Introvert 70%
Introverts are energized by spending quiet time alone or with a small group. They tend to be more reserved and thoughtful.

S - Observant 70%
Those with the Observant style are interested in observable facts and straightforward outcomes. They prefer to avoid layering too much interpretation on what they see.

F - Feeling 78%
Feelers tend to make decisions with their hearts; they are interested in how a decision will affect people, and whether it fits in with their values.

J - Judging 58%
Judgers appreciate structure and order; they like things planned, and dislike last-minute changes.

T - Turbulent 69%
Turbulent individuals are success-driven, perfectionistic, and eager to improve. They are always trying to counterbalance their self-doubts by achieving more.

Big Five Personality Test

The Big Five Personality Test measures the five personality factors that psychologists have determined are core to our personality makeup:

  1. Openness - How open a person is to new ideas and experiences
  2. Conscientiousness - How goal-directed, persistent, and organized a person is
  3. Extraversion - How much a person is energized by the outside world
  4. Agreeableness - How much a person puts others' interests and needs ahead of their own
  5. Neuroticism - How sensitive a person is to stress and negative emotional triggers

My Scores on the Big Five Personality Test are:
Openness: 65%
Conscientiousness: 81%
Extraversion: 27%
Agreeableness: 79%
Neuroticism: 67%

What do the results of these tests mean for you?

I feel the results of these online tests are accurate for me, so they are helpful in understanding the way I experience the world and interact with others. As a student, it’s helpful to know that I am a visual learner as there are steps I can take to improve my study, including taking notes during classes, watching videos on relevant topics, and reviewing information by re-writing it from memory. The Myers-Briggs and Big Five Personality tests provide a range of insights into my personality, including that I am introverted, enjoy supporting others, am conscientious, open and dedicated. They also show weaknesses such as repressing feelings, perfectionism and tending to blame myself when something goes wrong. The information provided by these tests is useful because it helps me be aware of my feelings, actions and behaviours so that I may cope better in a variety of situations.

How do you think these results may influence your behaviour in a team?

The results of my tests show there are both strengths and weaknesses for me when working in a team. As a Defender personality type, I am organised, reliable, and have good people skills, despite being mostly introverted. I am also good at listening to and supporting others which are great skills for teamwork. However, the test suggests that Defender’s strong sense of duty and innate altruism, mean it is often easy to become overloaded by others, for example, by taking on too much work. The results suggest that Defender’s need to know when to say no and stand up for themselves in order to maintain confidence and enthusiasm, and this ability could be important when completing work in a team.

How should you take this into account when forming a team?

In forming a team, it would be ideal for me to work with others who have some similar traits as me, such as being reliable, organised and hard-working. If I happened to be in a team where some members didn’t do their fair share of the work or were happy to leave things until the last minute, that would be very frustrating for me and I would find it tricky to deal with. The contradiction in my personality type of being introverted but also having good people skills and being open, means I can communicate well and get along with most other personality types. Therefore, my main considerations are to work with others who are going to contribute equally, be organised and reliable.
